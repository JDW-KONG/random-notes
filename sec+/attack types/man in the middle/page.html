Man in the Middle Attacks
- attacker intercepts traffic going between two hosts and then changes the information in the packets in real time
- the man in the middle can inject false information between hosts

- How can a bad guy watch you without knowing?
- man in the middle attacks
- sits between target and destination

- redirects your traffic
- then passes it on the the destination
- you never know that your traffic was redirected

- ARP poisoning
- ARP has no security
- operates at layer 2 of the OSI reference model
- uses MAC addresses
- must be done locally

- an attacker sends spoofed ARP messages to a local area network
- may allow an attacker to:
- intercept data frames on a network
- modify traffic
- stop all traffic
  • - can be prevented by using IPSec tunnels across your internal network


Man in the Browser
- a trojan is used to intercept and manipulate calls between the main application's executable (the browser) and its security mechanisms

- the most common objective of this attack is to commit financial fraud by manipulating the transactions of internet banking systems

- kerberos prevents replay attacks, as it uses updated sequence numbers and timestamps

- What if the middleman was on the same computer as the victim?
- the calls are coming from inside the browser
- malware/trojan does all of the proxy work

- huge advantage to the bad guys
- relatively easy to proxy encrypted traffic
- everything looks normal to the victim

- the man in the browser waits for you to log into your bank
- and the cleans you out


Padding Oracle On Downgraded Legacy Encryption
- POODLE attack

- exploits the use of SSL 3.0 on legacy systems

- a downgrade attack is when you abandon a higher level of security for an older, low-level security system
